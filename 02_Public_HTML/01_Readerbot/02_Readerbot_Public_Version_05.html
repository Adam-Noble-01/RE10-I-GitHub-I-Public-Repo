EX03 | The History Of Green Tea

This document provides a guide to green tea, including its origins, health benefits, production regions, and global significance as a commodity.

Green Tea Origins

Origins Of Green Tea

Green tea originated in China, where it has been cultivated and consumed for thousands of years. According to legend, it was discovered by Emperor Shen Nong in 2737 BC when leaves from a wild tea tree accidentally blew into his pot of boiling water. This serendipitous moment marks the start of tea consumption in China, eventually leading to the cultivation and development of various tea varieties.

When People Started Drinking Green Tea

Tea drinking began in China as a medicinal practice long before it became a popular beverage. By the Tang Dynasty (618–907 AD), tea had become widespread and integrated into daily Chinese life. It was introduced to Japan by Buddhist monks in the 8th century and later made its way to Europe in the 17th century, where it gained popularity due to its perceived health benefits.

Differences Between Green Tea And Regular Tea

Green tea differs from regular (black) tea primarily in the processing method. Green tea leaves undergo minimal oxidation, preserving their green colour and light flavour. Black tea, in contrast, is fully oxidised, giving it a darker colour and stronger taste. Green tea contains more antioxidants but less caffeine than black tea.

Health Benefits Of Green Tea

Green Tea And Wellness

Green tea is widely regarded for its numerous health benefits, attributed to its high content of antioxidants, polyphenols, and catechins. These natural compounds contribute to its reputation as a wellness beverage.

Key Health Benefits

  • Rich In Antioxidants: Green tea contains powerful antioxidants, particularly EGCG, which help to neutralise free radicals and reduce oxidative stress.
  • Heart Health: Regular consumption may reduce bad cholesterol (LDL), improve circulation, and lower blood pressure, reducing the risk of heart disease.
  • Brain Function: The combination of caffeine and L-theanine can improve focus, alertness, and memory while promoting relaxation.
  • Weight Loss: Green tea can enhance metabolism and assist in fat burning, making it popular in weight management.
  • Skin Health: The antioxidants in green tea help protect the skin from UV damage and promote anti-ageing effects.
  • Cancer Prevention: Some studies suggest green tea may lower the risk of certain cancers, such as breast and prostate cancers.

Regions Where Green Tea Is Popular

East Asia

  • China: Green tea originated in China, where it remains a central part of daily life and culture. Varieties such as Longjing and Biluochun are highly prized.
  • Japan: Known for its Matcha and Sencha, Japan has a deep-rooted green tea culture, with tea ceremonies playing a significant role in its traditions.
  • South Korea: Green tea is grown in Boseong, where its consumption is tied to health and well-being.

South Asia

  • India: Though primarily known for black tea, India is expanding its green tea production. Varieties from Assam and Darjeeling are gaining popularity.

Southeast Asia

  • Vietnam: Green tea is a staple in northern Vietnam, where it is enjoyed daily and has become an important export product.

Middle East & North Africa

  • Morocco: Green tea, particularly Moroccan Mint Tea, is a staple beverage, symbolising hospitality and shared community experiences.

Western Countries

  • United States: Green tea has surged in popularity due to its health benefits, with Matcha becoming particularly trendy.
  • Europe: Green tea consumption is rising in Western Europe, driven by health-conscious consumers. Russia and other Eastern European countries also have a growing appreciation for green tea.

Where Green Tea Is Produced

Major Producing Regions

  • China: The world’s largest producer, responsible for over 50% of global green tea. Key regions include Zhejiang, Anhui, and Fujian.
  • Japan: Known for high-quality green tea, particularly Matcha and Sencha, with production concentrated in Shizuoka and Uji.
  • South Korea: Boseong is renowned for its green tea fields and production of quality teas.
  • India: Growing green tea production in regions like Darjeeling and Assam to meet increasing global demand.
  • Vietnam: A major green tea exporter, with the Thai Nguyen Province being a key production area.
  • Taiwan: Known for high-quality teas, with smaller-scale green tea production.

Emerging Producers

Countries like Sri Lanka, Kenya, and parts of Africa are expanding their green tea production due to increasing global demand.

Green Tea: Global Prices & How It’s Traded

Green Tea As A Commodity

Green tea is traded globally, with prices fluctuating based on quality, demand, and production. The market is dominated by countries like China and Japan, with green tea sold through auctions, direct contracts, and retail channels.

Pricing Factors

  • Quality Grading: Green tea is graded by origin and processing methods, with premium varieties commanding higher prices.
  • Supply & Demand: Adverse weather in tea-producing regions and increasing global demand, especially in health-conscious markets, affect prices.
  • Export Markets: Key importers include the United States, Russia, and Europe, where demand has surged in recent years.

Trade Mechanisms

Green tea is sold through auctions, with large volumes exported via direct contracts between producers and suppliers. In addition to traditional trading, e-commerce platforms have become significant distribution channels, especially for Matcha and specialty teas.

Traditions And Cultural Significance Of Green Tea

East Asia

Green tea plays a significant role in the daily lives of people in China and Japan, where it is linked with meditation, ceremonial practices, and hospitality.

North Africa

In Morocco, green tea, particularly in the form of Mint Tea, is a symbol of generosity and community. It is a staple of social gatherings, deeply embedded in Moroccan culture.

Western Influence

In recent years, the global popularity of green tea has expanded beyond its traditional roots, with Western nations incorporating Matcha and other forms of green tea into modern cuisine, wellness practices, and cultural trends.
